About Rainwater Harvesting

Out of the 30 million hectares of cultivable land in Pakistan, nearly 12 million hectares (about 40%) are classified as dryland areas. In these regions, livelihoods are entirely dependent on rainfall, as groundwater is either unavailable or too saline for use. This makes rainwater harvesting a crucial practice for sustaining human and livestock populations. Rainwater harvesting is not just a traditional practiceâ€”it is a sustainable approach to conserving water for future generations. It plays an important role in addressing water scarcity, supporting agricultural production, and maintaining the ecological balance in arid and semi-arid regions.

Rainfall is the only reliable source of water in these areas, and the collected rainwater is stored in natural depressions known locally as tobas or in underground dugwells called kunds. Initially, these water reservoirs were sufficient to meet the needs of small, localized communities and their livestock. However, over time, population growth and livestock numbers have significantly increased, placing additional strain on these limited water resources. Many of the tobass have also silted up, reducing their capacity to store rainwater and making it more difficult for rural communities to survive during dry spells.

As a result, when local water sources dry up, communitiesâ€”often nomadicâ€”must migrate in search of water for themselves and their livestock. This migration disrupts livelihoods and increases the vulnerability of both humans and animals to malnutrition and disease. For instance, in the Cholistan Desert, approximately 0.1 million people depend on about 2 million livestock, while in the Thar Desert, around 1.5 million people rely on nearly 6 million livestock. Without adequate water storage facilities, these regions face severe hardships, especially during prolonged droughts.

To address these challenges, the development of additional rainwater harvesting structures such as small dams, ponds, check dams, and improved tobass is essential. These initiatives can help secure water availability throughout the year, promote rangeland development, and improve the resilience of local communities. By adopting modern rainwater harvesting techniques and integrating them with traditional knowledge, Pakistan can improve its water security and promote sustainable development in vulnerable regions.

Key Benefits of Rainwater Harvesting

  • Provides a reliable water source for domestic, agricultural, and livestock use.
  • Reduces dependency on groundwater, helping to prevent over-extraction and depletion.
  • Improves soil moisture and increases crop productivity in dryland farming.
  • Helps in flood mitigation by reducing surface runoff during heavy rains.
  • Enhances biodiversity and supports reforestation in arid regions.

Common Rainwater Harvesting Methods

  • Surface runoff harvesting: Collecting rainwater from open fields, roads, or slopes and directing it into ponds or reservoirs.
  • Rooftop rainwater collection: Storing water from roofs through gutters into storage tanks for household use.
  • Percolation pits and recharge wells: Allowing rainwater to seep underground to recharge groundwater levels.
  • Check dams and bunds: Constructing barriers across small streams to slow water flow and promote infiltration.
